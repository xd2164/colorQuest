📋 Facilitation Guide · Color Conquest

Teaching AI by Design · Design–Create–Reflect (DCR) Framework · AI4K12 Five Big Ideas

Suggested timing for a 40-minute class period

Adjust freely — the Reflect phase is the most important and should not be rushed.

0–2 min
Welcome + DCR framing. Tell students: "Today we're going to design a learning experiment, play a game, and then reflect on what we learned about AI." Briefly name the three phases.
2–5 min
Role selection and difficulty (Design phase). Students pick their archetype. Ask: "What are you curious about? Pick the difficulty that matches that question."
5–8 min
Rules orientation — read aloud or demonstrate on a projector. Point out the AI Coach panel and ask students to pay attention to the Big Idea annotations as they play.
8–23 min
Gameplay (Create phase). Circulate. Prompt: "What is the AI doing? Does that surprise you?" For teacher mode: switch archetypes mid-game to trigger comparison.
23–28 min
Reflection journal — silent, individual (Reflect phase). Students complete all 3 prompts. This is time-protected: do not use it for discussion.
28–33 min
Small group share (2–3 students). Each person shares one thing they noticed about the AI's strategy. Groups identify the biggest point of disagreement.
33–40 min
Whole-class debrief using scenario discussion questions (see tabs). Close with the DCR Debrief questions to connect game behavior to real AI systems.
🐢 Greedy Agent
Big Idea 2: Representation & Reasoning · CS concept: Greedy algorithm / reward maximization
Opening question
"What rule do you think the Greedy AI is following? Try to say it in one sentence." → Guide toward: "It always picks the color that gives it the most tiles right now."
Probe: Is greedy always optimal?
"Can you think of a moment in the game when the greedy choice actually hurt the AI? What did it miss?" → Students may notice: the AI sometimes gains 4 tiles but gives the player access to a large cluster next turn.
Bridge to real AI
"Social media recommendation feeds often work like greedy agents — they show you whatever gets the most immediate clicks. What are the long-term consequences of that design choice?"
Closing design question
"If you were going to fix the Greedy Agent, what information would you give it? What would it need to 'know' to make a better decision?"
🔭 Predictive Agent
Big Ideas 1 & 2: Perception + Reasoning · CS concept: Minimax / lookahead search
Opening question
"Did you notice the AI making a move that seemed weak but then made sense a turn later? What was it planning?" → Validate student observations even if they don't use the technical term "lookahead."
Probe: What does lookahead require?
"For the Predictive Agent to plan 2 turns ahead, what does it need to know? What does it still not know about what YOU will do?" → Connect to uncertainty, state space, computational cost of planning further ahead.
Bridge to real AI
"Navigation apps plan your route ahead of time but still don't know about accidents that just happened. How is that like the Predictive Agent? What are the limits of planning?"
Closing design question
"How many turns ahead should an AI plan? Is planning further ahead always better? Are there situations where an agent that plans too much is actually worse?"
🧬 Adaptive Agent
Big Idea 3: Learning · CS concept: Online learning algorithm / behavioral tracking
Opening question
"Did you change your strategy as the game went on? Did you feel like the AI was 'watching' you? When did you first notice it adapting?" → This question often produces strong reactions — students find it unsettling in a productive way.
Probe: What is the AI learning?
"The Adaptive Agent tracks which colors you pick most often. How is that similar to what a spam filter does? A recommendation algorithm? Your phone's autocorrect?" → Build the transfer to familiar systems.
Probe: Being 'learned'
"How did it feel to have your behavior tracked and responded to? What's the difference between an AI that learns your habits and one that learns your goals? Which would you trust more?"
Bridge to real AI
"Netflix, Spotify, and TikTok all use adaptive algorithms. What happens when the algorithm's model of you is wrong? Can it ever be corrected? Who decides?"
Closing design question
"If you were designing an adaptive system for something you care about — a study tool, a health app, a news feed — what would you want it to learn about you? What should it never learn?"
💤 Passive Agent
Big Idea 5: Societal Impact · CS concept: Random baseline / fairness vs. optimization
Opening question
"The Passive Agent moved mostly at random. How did that change the game? Did it feel fairer? Or just easier? Is easier the same as fairer?" → Push students to distinguish difficulty, fairness, and randomness.
Probe: When is randomness a feature?
"Some researchers argue that adding randomness to AI systems makes them less biased — that a less-optimized system might be fairer in practice. When do you think that argument holds? When does it break down?"
Bridge to real AI
"Imagine a college admissions algorithm. Would you want a Greedy version (maximizes some outcome), a Predictive version (models your future performance), an Adaptive version (learns from past applicants), or a Passive version (with some randomness built in)? What would each decision optimize for? Who benefits?"
Closing design question
"If you could set one rule the algorithm has to follow — that it's not allowed to break, no matter what — what would that rule be? What value is that rule protecting?"

① Design phase — revisit

"You chose your difficulty at the start. What were you trying to learn? Did your experiment work? What would you change about the design for a second round?"

② Create phase — what happened

"What happened in the game that you didn't predict? How did you adjust? What did the AI do that forced you to think differently?"

③ Reflect phase — transfer

"If you were going to explain what you learned about AI to a family member, in one sentence, what would you say?" · "What's one AI system you interact with every day that probably uses one of these four strategies? Which one?"

Big Ideas connection check

Ask students to vote (show of hands or slips): Which Big Idea was most visible in your game today? Tally the room. Discuss why different archetypes surface different Big Ideas.

Assessment options

Exit ticket: "Name the AI archetype you played against, name one real AI system that works similarly, and state one question you still have." · Portfolio: Export the session report (includes reflection responses) — submit with a 1-paragraph summary of what they changed about their strategy and why. · Discussion grade: Quality of one contribution during debrief, scored on claim + evidence from game experience.
